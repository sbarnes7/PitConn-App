Compressive Testing of an IVC Filter Medical Device

Inferior vena cava (IVC) filters are implantable medical devices designed to prevent blood clots from moving through bloodstreams and into lungs. These devices are implanted into the human body and experience a significant amount of mechanical loading due to compression of the vessel on the device with every heartbeat. Therefore, durability testing for these and similar devices is crucial for ensuring device performance and safety over the lifetime of the device, which is many years and decades. Tests such as these are important to understand materials and device failure limits and to validate mechanical durability of final device designs. Both are useful for regulatory submittals and approvals by FDA and similar regulatory bodies.

In this test example, a modified hook vena cava filter was placed inside a length of silicone tubing, simulating the filter inside a vein. This was placed in an ElectroForce 3200 test instrument which was used to physically apply the loading to the tube and device. An ElectroForce 5500, DMA 3200 or 3300 would yield similar results. The 10Hz loading profile was a cyclic compression sequence of 300 cycles of 5% compression to the filter followed by 50 cycles of 20% compression. That loading combination was repeated over 1 million times with the goal of testing for 10 years of heartbeats, or 400 Million cycles.

Even when accelerated at 10Hz, this test will last 15 months. Therefore, these tests are often accelerated even more to reduce testing times to 3-6 months. The high-speed performance and reliability of the ElectroForce test instruments is a perfect match for high cycle tests like these.
